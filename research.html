Income, Consumption and Savings during COVID-19
(with Manuel Adelino and Miguel Ferreira)
This paper examines the response of households' savings and consumption to a large-scale mortgage forbearance programs. We use data from a large Portuguese bank that combine individual credit and debit card transactions, transfers into the bank, and account-level balances to measure monthly income, consumption expenditure, and savings. We show that postponing mortgage payments during the Covid-19 pandemic had a large impact on both consumption and savings, but with heterogeneous impacts across income and wealth groups. Low-income and low-wealth individuals in forbearance increase spending by 74 cents per euro of postponed loan payments, corresponding to an average consumption above the pre-pandemic level. In contrast, households in the high-income, high-wealth group increase savings by 79 cents per euro of postponed payments. Our findings suggest that the combination of forbearance programs and income supplements may not be necessary to avoid widespread loan defaults during a crisis.

Do Specialized Distress Investors Undermine Upstream Lending?
(with Fernando Anjos and Irem Demirci)
We extend the canonical model of corporate finance under moral hazard to allow for specialized distress investors (SDIs) and ask whether and how these financiers create negative externalities for upstream lending. In our model SDIs potentially participate in the firm at an interim stage, only following poor performance. Under certain conditions the presence of SDIs can per se make (theoretically) positive-NPV projects impossible to fund ex ante, by inadvertently making it more expensive to incentivize the borrower and/or by reducing the initial lender's ex post surplus conditional on poor performance. Furthermore, SDIs can reduce a project's ex ante NPV, even when they do not affect borrower incentives nor expropriate lenders ex post. Finally, we also show how SDI presence can compromise the initial lender's ability to develop and maintain a reputation.

How costly is default around the world? Evidence from structural estimation
I estimate how the expected cost of default varies across 14 OECD countries. To infer the magnitude of cross-country differences, I consider a dynamic capital structure model, where firms face corporate taxes, costly external financing and costly default and then apply simulated method of moments to the model.
